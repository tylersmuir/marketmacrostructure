Market Macrostructure

The institutions that hold assets shape their prices.

Over 50% of equity fund assets are now passive. Central banks hold 40% of GDP in bonds. Leveraged intermediaries amplify every crisis. These forces change everything about how markets work — and this is the research frontier.

Explore the Agenda Read the Paper

What is Market Macrostructure?

Market macrostructure studies the broad organization of financial markets — who the key players are, how they form portfolios, and how this shapes asset prices. Unlike microstructure (trading mechanics), macrostructure examines the big picture of institutional demand and equilibrium.

Who

Who are the investors? Households, pension funds, mutual funds, ETFs, hedge funds, central banks, insurance companies — and how large are they?

How

How do they form portfolios? What demand functions, constraints, mandates, and benchmarks shape their behavior?

What

What are the price implications? How do shifts in investor composition change risk premia, volatility, and market efficiency?

“Prefer being approximately right rather than rigorously wrong.”

The macrostructure approach: let what institutions actually do guide the models, not the reverse.

The Key Insight

Traditional View

E[R] = f(Risk, Preferences)

Asset prices are determined by risk and representative-agent preferences. Institutions are a veil — money flows through them but they don't independently affect prices.

Macrostructure View

E[R] = f(Risk, Preferences, Institutions)

Institutional frictions, constraints, and mandates create demand that affects equilibrium prices beyond what fundamentals alone would predict.

When Does Macrostructure Matter?

1

Imperfect Offset

Households don't perfectly undo institutions' trades — due to trading costs, inertia, limited expertise, or behavioral frictions.

+
2

Demand Shifts

Institutions' demand changes — from crises, regulation, policy shifts, or structural trends like the rise of passive investing.

=

Macrostructure affects asset prices

Three Forces Reshaping Markets

The macrostructure framework illuminates three forces with quantitatively large, measurable effects on asset prices.

Financial Intermediaries

300+ basis point spike in corporate bond spreads, March 2020

When intermediary health deteriorates, their risk-bearing capacity falls, amplifying price movements across asset classes. Crises hit intermediated markets hardest — and the effects persist through regulation and balance sheet constraints.

Key Papers
  • He & Krishnamurthy (2013) — Intermediary asset pricing theory
  • Adrian, Etula & Muir (2014) — Leverage factor prices the cross-section
  • Haddad & Muir (2021) — Intermediaries matter for aggregate prices
  • Baron & Muir (2022) — 150 years of intermediary balance sheets

Rise of Passive Investing

53% of U.S. equity fund assets are now passive (crossed 50% in 2024)

Passive funds hold the market portfolio regardless of prices — they don't respond to mispricing. This reduces aggregate demand elasticity and concentrates the burden of price discovery on the shrinking pool of active investors. Markets become measurably more inelastic.

Key Papers
  • Haddad, Huebner & Loualiche (2024) — How competitive is the stock market?
  • Gabaix & Koijen (2021) — The inelastic markets hypothesis
  • Pavlova & Sikorskaya (2023) — Benchmarking intensity
  • Chinco & Sammon (2024) — Passive share and market efficiency

Central Bank Asset Purchases

~40% of GDP held in central bank balance sheets (US, UK, Euro area, Japan)

When central banks buy assets through QE, they remove supply from private markets, compressing yields and risk premia. QE announcements moved Treasury yields 100–150 basis points. The trading rule itself matters: investors anticipate state-contingent purchases, so yields fall even before the Fed buys.

Key Papers
  • Krishnamurthy & Vissing-Jorgensen (2011) — Effects of QE on interest rates
  • Vayanos & Vila (2021) — Preferred-habitat term structure theory
  • Haddad, Moreira & Muir (2024) — Whatever it takes? Conditional policy
  • Haddad, Moreira & Muir (2025) — Asset purchase rules

Who Holds the Assets?

The composition of asset holders has shifted dramatically. These interactive charts show the data — sourced from the Federal Reserve Z.1 Financial Accounts. Hover to see exact values.

Key Observations

  • Equities: Direct household holdings fell from 86% (1960) to 40% (2023), while mutual funds/ETFs rose from 3% to 28%.
  • Treasuries & Agency/MBS: The Fed became a dominant holder through QE, expanding from ~10% of Treasuries pre-2008 to 24% by 2020, and from 2% of Agency/MBS to 30%.
  • Corporate Bonds: Insurance companies' share fell from 45% (1960) to 15% (2023), replaced by mutual funds — creating new fragility through outflow-driven selling.
  • Households: Direct household holdings in fixed income are remarkably low: just 4% of Treasuries, 1% of Agency/MBS, and 7% of corporate bonds as of 2023.

The Playbook: How to Do This Research

Market macrostructure research combines three complementary approaches. The most powerful work uses them together.

1
Pick a market. Identify the key players.
→
2
Measure their demand. Understand what drives it.
→
3
Trace the equilibrium. Run counterfactuals.

Natural Experiments

Exploit shocks to institutional demand — index additions, regulatory changes, QE announcements — to identify causal effects on prices.

Shleifer (1986) Du, Tepper & Verdelhan (2018) Muir (2017)

Equilibrium Models

Build models with rich investor heterogeneity, frictions, and dynamics to understand mechanisms and run policy counterfactuals.

He & Krishnamurthy (2013) Vayanos & Vila (2021) Brunnermeier & Sannikov (2014)

Demand Estimation

Use holdings data to estimate flexible demand systems across investor types. Data-driven, rich heterogeneity, quantitative counterfactuals.

Koijen & Yogo (2019) Gabaix & Koijen (2021) Haddad, Huebner & Loualiche (2024)

The data and tools exist. The research frontier is accessible. Pick a market and start.

Open Questions

The frontier of market macrostructure research. Each of these is an area where new work can make first-order contributions.

🎯

Welfare & Efficiency

When do macrostructure effects represent inefficiencies versus rational risk sharing? Are passive-driven price distortions welfare-reducing, or do they reflect efficient delegation?

🔄

Dynamic Macrostructure

How do players and frictions evolve endogenously? Long-run trends (passive growth) versus crisis dynamics (intermediary amplification) — can we build models that capture both?

🌍

Global & Emerging Markets

Different institutions, different frictions. How does macrostructure differ in emerging markets? What role do sovereign wealth funds, state banks, and capital controls play?

⚖️

Market Design & Regulation

How should regulation account for macrostructure? What are optimal central bank purchase strategies? How do leverage rules reshape who bears risk?

🌱

ESG & Sustainable Investing

Shifts in demand for ESG assets affect prices and firm investment. How large are these effects? Do they achieve their intended goals?

💱

FX & International Finance

Exchange rate determination through the lens of institutional demand. How do FX interventions, carry trades, and global intermediary networks shape currency markets?
