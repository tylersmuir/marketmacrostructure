Market Macrostructure

Understanding How Institutional Investors Shape Asset Prices

A research framework for PhD students exploring the intersection of institutional finance, asset pricing, and market structure.

Explore the Framework

What is Market Macrostructure?

Market macrostructure studies how the composition and behavior of institutional investors affects asset prices at the aggregate level. Unlike microstructure (which focuses on trading mechanics), macrostructure examines the "big picture" of who holds assets and how their demand functions shape equilibrium prices.

Who

Who are the investors? Households, pension funds, mutual funds, ETFs, hedge funds, central banks, insurance companies.

How

How do they form portfolios? What are their demand functions? How do constraints, mandates, and benchmarks shape behavior?

What

What are the price implications? How do shifts in investor composition affect risk premia, volatility, and market efficiency?

The Key Insight

Traditional View

E[R] = f(Risk, Preferences)

Asset prices determined by risk and representative agent preferences. Institutions are a "veil."

Macrostructure View

E[R] = f(Risk, Preferences, Institutions)

Institutional frictions and constraints create demand that affects equilibrium prices beyond fundamentals.

"The identity and behavior of the marginal investor matters for asset prices."

The Theoretical Framework

The macrostructure framework builds on a tractable demand-based model that allows us to aggregate diverse investor types and understand equilibrium price formation.

Tractable Aggregation

Linear demands allow simple aggregation across heterogeneous investors with different risk tolerances and beliefs.

Compositional Effects

Changes in who holds assets (e.g., shift to passive) affect prices even with fixed total wealth.

Demand Elasticity

The aggregate demand elasticity A determines how much prices must adjust to clear markets.

Three Major Applications

The macrostructure framework illuminates three important phenomena in modern financial markets.

Financial Intermediaries

When intermediary health deteriorates, their risk-bearing capacity falls, amplifying price movements.

Key Papers
  • He & Krishnamurthy (2013)
  • Adrian, Etula & Muir (2014)
  • Haddad & Muir (2021)

Rise of Passive Investing

Passive funds don't respond to prices, reducing aggregate demand elasticity and increasing the burden on remaining active investors.

Key Papers
  • Haddad, Huebner & Loualiche (2024)
  • Ben-David et al. (2022)
  • Pavlova & Sikorskaya (2023)

Central Bank Asset Purchases

When central banks buy assets (QE), they remove supply from private markets, pushing up prices and compressing yields.

Key Papers
  • Vayanos & Vila (2021)
  • Krishnamurthy & Vissing-Jorgensen (2011)
  • Haddad, Moreira & Muir (2024, 2025)

About This Resource

This website provides an introduction to the market macrostructure framework for PhD students interested in research at the intersection of institutional finance and asset pricing.

The content is based on the survey paper "Market Macrostructure: Institutions and Asset Prices" by Valentin Haddad and Tyler Muir, published in the Annual Review of Financial Economics (2021).
